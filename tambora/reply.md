On the Tambora-Yunnan famine connection: As far as I know, one of the earliest Chinese scholarly works to explicitly link the 1815 Tambora eruption to the severe 1815–1817 famine in Yunnan (the Jiaqing Great Famine) came from Fudan University researchers. It was published in 2005 in the *Fudan Journal (Social Sciences)*—you can see the English abstract here: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/285022868_A_serious_famine_in_Yunnan_1815-1817_and_the_eruption_of_Tambora_Volcano. 

Regarding the Zhong Weimin reference I mentioned from Tsinghua University: Here's the direct link to the article—it's the 2010 English version in *Frontiers of History in China*: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11462-010-0004-0. The full title is "The roles of tea and opium in early economic globalization: A perspective on China’s crisis in the 19th century."

A final note about “A Sigh for Autumn Rain”: The version most educated Chinese readers know (and the one that came to mind for me) is from Du Fu. He actually wrote three poems under this title. Here's a link to an English translation of one (the third, I believe): https://100tangpoems.wordpress.com/2023/10/25/autumn-rain/. The translation isn't perfect, but you can see the similar melancholic vibe—especially lines like "Autumn comes and I haven’t seen the light of day / Mud and mire, when will it dry?" It echoes the despair and endless rain in Li Yuyang's version you discuss.
