That said, I feel compelled to share one point of concern regarding the timeline and scale of opium cultivation in Yunnan and the opium connection with Tambora eruption. I offer this in the spirit of constructive feedback, drawing from my familiarity with Chinese history and culture.

In the text, you describe (paraphrased):

> “Beginning in 1820, however, only two years after the end of the Tambora-driven famine, Chinese rulers in Peking were startled to receive reports from faraway Yunnan of a sudden explosion in opium production there… by 1840—during the first Opium War with Britain—Yunnan was the acknowledged heart of domestic opium production in China.”

This timeline and the description of a “sudden explosion” in 1820, with Yunnan already the “acknowledged heart” by 1840, do not align with the mainstream historical scholarship on opium cultivation in Yunnan —particularly in light of the book's opening question in Chapter Five: “What caused Yunnan’s sudden transformation, in less than two decades, from a grain-producing province well integrated with the empire’s agricultural system to a rogue narco-state in thrall to the international drug trade?” and the subsequent claim that what follows is a “probable scenario” driven by the Tambora famine. While the famine of 1816–1818 likely served as an important accelerator for small farmers' shift toward high-value cash crops like opium, the major commercial takeoff and Yunnan's emergence as the dominant domestic producer occurred much later, primarily in the post-Opium Wars era (especially the 1870s–1890s).

Here is a more accurate timeline based on established Chinese and international research:

1. **Pre-1840 (including the Tambora period 1816–1818)**  
   Opium poppy cultivation in Yunnan was limited to scattered, small-scale planting, mainly in remote border areas among ethnic minority communities (e.g., near Burma and Vietnam). It was mostly for local use or small trade, not yet a large-scale commercial industry. There was no “sudden explosion” in 1820, nor was Yunnan a major concern for the central court at that time.

2. **1840s–1860s (post-Opium Wars)**  
   After the First Opium War (1840–1842) and Second Opium War (1856–1860), imported foreign opium flooded China, causing severe silver outflow. This led to the policy shift toward “yitu diyiang” 以土抵洋 (using domestic opium to offset foreign imports). Yunnan planting began to grow noticeably during this period, but it was still secondary to provinces such as Sichuan, Guizhou, Shaanxi, and Gansu.

3. **1870s–1890s (Guangxu reign peak)**  
   Under the “yujin yuzheng” 寓禁于征 policy (nominal prohibition combined with taxation), Yunnan’s opium industry entered its golden age. Yunnan “Yuntu” (云土) became the most famous and highest-quality domestic opium, with cultivation area reaching an estimated 700,000–1,000,000 mu (≈46,000–66,000 hectares) at peak. By the late 19th century, Yunnan was widely recognized as the largest and most important domestic opium-producing province in China.

While the Tambora-induced famine of 1816–1818 likely acted as an important accelerator for the shift to high-value cash crops (including opium) among Yunnan’s small farmers, the “sudden explosion” and Yunnan’s status as the “acknowledged heart” of Chinese domestic opium production did not occur in the 1820s, nor was it fully established by 1840. These developments belong mainly to the post-Opium Wars period, especially the 1870s–1890s.
