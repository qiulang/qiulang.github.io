Subject: A Factual Correction Regarding the Timeline of Opium Cultivation in Yunnan in "Tambora"

Dear Dr. Gillen D'Arcy Wood,

I hope this email finds you well. First of all, I would like to express my appreciation for your book *Tambora: The Eruption That Changed the World*. It is an ambitious and engaging work that successfully connects a single geological event to a wide range of global social, cultural, literary, and economic consequences.

However, I feel compelled to point out what I believe is a significant inaccuracy in the section discussing the rise of opium production in Yunnan following the Tambora crisis (pp. [insert page number if known], or the chapter on Asia/China).

In the book, you write (paraphrased):

> “Beginning in 1820, however, only two years after the end of the Tambora-driven famine, Chinese rulers in Peking were startled to receive reports from faraway Yunnan of a sudden explosion in opium production there… by 1840—during the first Opium War with Britain—Yunnan was the acknowledged heart of domestic opium production in China.”

This timeline and characterization of “sudden explosion” in 1820 and Yunnan becoming the “acknowledged heart” by 1840 do not align with the mainstream historical scholarship on opium cultivation in Yunnan. The major expansion and Yunnan’s rise to dominance occurred much later.

Here is a more accurate timeline based on established Chinese and international scholarship:

1. **Pre-1840 (including 1816–1820 Tambora period)**  
   Opium poppy cultivation in Yunnan existed mainly as scattered, small-scale planting in border regions (especially among ethnic minority communities in the southwest and along trade routes with Burma and Vietnam). It was mostly for local use or limited trade, not yet a large-scale commercial crop.  
   There was no “sudden explosion” in 1820, nor was Yunnan a major concern for the central court at that time.

   → Reference: Qin Heping 秦和平, *Yunnan Yapian Wenti Yanjiu* (云南鸦片问题研究), 2001; also Huang Fanghui 黄芳辉, “Qingdai Yunnan Yapian Zhongzhi yu Difang Shehui” (清代云南鸦片种植与地方社会), 2015.

2. **1840s–1860s (post-Opium Wars)**  
   After the First Opium War (1840–1842) and Second Opium War (1856–1860), imported foreign opium flooded China, causing severe silver outflow. This led to the policy shift toward “yitu diyiang” 以土抵洋 (using domestic opium to offset foreign imports).  
   Yunnan planting began to grow noticeably during this period, but it was still secondary to provinces such as Sichuan, Guizhou, Shaanxi, and Gansu.

   → Reference: David Bello, *Opium and the Limits of Empire in China*, 2005; also Zhong Weimin 钟伟民, *Yapian yu Zhongguo* (鸦片与中国), 2004.

3. **1870s–1890s (Guangxu reign peak)**  
   Under the “yujin yuzheng” 寓禁于征 policy (nominal prohibition combined with taxation), Yunnan’s opium industry entered its golden age.  
   Yunnan “Yuntu” (云土) became the most famous and highest-quality domestic opium, with cultivation area reaching an estimated 700,000–1,000,000 mu (≈46,000–66,000 hectares) at peak.  
   By the late 19th century, Yunnan was widely recognized as the largest and most important domestic opium-producing province in China.

   → Reference: Qin Heping, ibid.; also Zhou Yongming 周永明, *Between the State and the Market: The Chinese Opium Trade in Modern Yunnan*, 1999; and reports from British consular officials in Yunnan (e.g., 1880s–1890s Imperial Maritime Customs reports).

In short, while the Tambora-induced famine of 1816–1818 likely acted as an important accelerator for the shift to high-value cash crops (including opium) among Yunnan’s small farmers, the “sudden explosion” and Yunnan’s status as the “acknowledged heart” of Chinese domestic opium production did not occur in the 1820s, nor was it fully established by 1840. These developments belong mainly to the post-Opium Wars period, especially the 1870s–1890s.

I understand that popular history books sometimes compress timelines for narrative flow, but this particular compression alters the causal sequence and historical accuracy quite significantly. I would be grateful if you could consider this point for any future editions or corrections.
